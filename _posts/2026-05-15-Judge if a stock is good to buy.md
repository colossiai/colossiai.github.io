**How to judge if a stock is good to buy?**

A good stock is usually a **good business at a reasonable price**.
You can judge it from 5 major areas:

---

# 1. Understand the Business

Ask:

* Does the company make money consistently?
* Is the business easy to understand?
* Does it have strong products or advantages?
* Will people still need it in 5–10 years?

Examples of strong business types:

* AI infrastructure
* Cloud software
* Semiconductor leaders
* Payment networks
* Healthcare leaders

Examples:

* NVIDIA
* Microsoft
* Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company

---

# 2. Check Revenue & Profit Growth

Good companies usually grow steadily.

Look for:

* Revenue growth
* Earnings growth (EPS)
* Free cash flow growth

Simple guideline:

* Revenue growth > 10% yearly is good
* EPS growing consistently is excellent

Bad sign:

* Revenue growing but profits shrinking

---

# 3. Check Financial Strength

Important things:

### Debt

* Too much debt = dangerous
* Prefer manageable debt

### Cash

* Large cash reserves are safer

### Margins

Higher profit margins usually mean:

* Strong pricing power
* Competitive advantage

---

# 4. Valuation — Is It Too Expensive?

Even great companies can be bad buys if overpriced.

Common metrics:

| Metric    | Meaning                |
| --------- | ---------------------- |
| PE        | Price vs earnings      |
| PEG       | PE adjusted for growth |
| PS        | Price vs sales         |
| FCF Yield | Cash generation value  |

General ideas:

* Lower PE ≠ automatically cheap
* High-growth companies often deserve higher PE
* PEG around 1–2 is often reasonable

Example:

* A company growing 30% yearly with PE 35 may be cheaper than a slow company with PE 20.

---

# 5. Competitive Advantage (“Moat”)

The best stocks often dominate their industry.

Strong moat examples:

* Brand
* Network effects
* Technology leadership
* Switching costs
* Scale advantages

Examples:

* Apple → ecosystem moat
* Visa → network moat
* Amazon → scale moat

---

# Simple Beginner Checklist

A stock is often attractive when:

✅ Business is understandable
✅ Revenue & profit growing
✅ Strong balance sheet
✅ Industry leader
✅ Long-term demand exists
✅ Valuation not absurdly expensive
✅ Management executes well

---

# Warning Signs

Avoid companies with:

* Constant losses
* Huge debt
* Declining revenue
* Dilution from too many new shares
* Hype without profits
* Fraud concerns
* “Too good to be true” promises

---

# Different Styles of Investing

### Growth Investing

Buy fast-growing companies.

Examples:

* AI
* Cloud
* Software
* Semiconductors

Usually:

* Higher PE
* Higher risk
* Higher upside

---

### Value Investing

Buy undervalued companies.

Look for:

* Low valuation
* Stable business
* Temporary market fear

---

### Dividend Investing

Focus on steady income.

Look for:

* Stable cash flow
* Reliable dividends
* Defensive industries

---

# One Very Important Rule

A great company is NOT always a great stock.

Example:

* Amazing company
* But stock already priced for perfection
  → future returns may disappoint

Price matters.

---

# Practical Framework

Before buying any stock, ask:

1. What does the company do?
2. How does it make money?
3. Why is it better than competitors?
4. Is revenue/profit growing?
5. Is debt manageable?
6. Is valuation reasonable?
7. Can it still grow in 5 years?
8. What could destroy the business?

If you can answer these clearly, you probably understand the investment.

Let’s use a few real tech companies to see how professionals think about valuation metrics in practice.

# 1. PE Ratio (Price/Earnings)

Formula:

[
PE = \frac{Stock\ Price}{EPS}
]

Or:
[
PE = \frac{Market\ Cap}{Net\ Income}
]

---

## Example: Microsoft

Suppose:

* Stock price = $500
* EPS = $15

Then:

[
PE = 500 / 15 = 33.3
]

Meaning:

> Investors pay $33 for every $1 of annual earnings.

---

## How professionals interpret this

### High PE can mean:

* Strong future growth expected
* Premium quality business
* AI tailwinds
* Strong moat

### Low PE can mean:

* Cheap opportunity
  OR
* Business problems

PE alone is NEVER enough.

---

# 2. PEG Ratio (PE adjusted for Growth)

Formula:

[
PEG = \frac{PE}{EPS\ Growth\ Rate}
]

---

## Example: NVIDIA

Suppose:

* PE = 45
* EPS growth expected = 55%

Then:

[
PEG = 45 / 55 = 0.82
]

This may actually be attractive.

Why?

Because earnings are growing extremely fast.

---

## Professional interpretation

| PEG | Interpretation                 |
| --- | ------------------------------ |
| < 1 | Potentially undervalued growth |
| 1–2 | Reasonable                     |
| > 2 | Expensive unless exceptional   |

---

# Important Insight

A fast-growing company can deserve a very high PE.

Example:

* PE 50 with 60% growth
  may be CHEAPER than:
* PE 15 with 5% growth

This is why growth investors love PEG.

---

# 3. DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)

Professionals LOVE DCF because:

> A stock is worth the future cash it can generate.

---

## Simplified DCF Example

Suppose Microsoft generates:

| Year | Free Cash Flow |
| ---- | -------------- |
| 2026 | $100B          |
| 2027 | $110B          |
| 2028 | $121B          |

Then investors:

1. Forecast many years ahead
2. Discount future cash back to today

Because:

* Future money is worth less than current money

---

## Simplified formula

[
DCF = \sum \frac{FCF}{(1+r)^n}
]

Where:

* FCF = free cash flow
* r = discount rate
* n = years

---

# What professionals care about in DCF

DCF is VERY sensitive to:

* Growth assumptions
* Margin assumptions
* Discount rate

Small changes can massively change valuation.

---

# Example of DCF thinking

### Bull case:

* AI boosts cloud demand
* Margins expand
* Revenue compounds 15%

### Bear case:

* AI spending slows
* Competition increases
* Margins compress

Professionals model BOTH.

---

# 4. EV/EBITDA

Very common in institutional investing.

---

## Formula

[
EV = MarketCap + Debt - Cash
]

[
EV/EBITDA = \frac{Enterprise\ Value}{EBITDA}
]

---

## Why professionals use it

PE ignores debt.

EV/EBITDA accounts for:

* Debt
* Cash
* Capital structure

This makes comparisons cleaner.

---

## Example: Broadcom

Suppose:

* Market cap = $900B
* Debt = $70B
* Cash = $15B

Then:

[
EV = 900 + 70 - 15 = 955B
]

Suppose EBITDA = $42B

Then:

[
EV/EBITDA = 955 / 42 = 22.7
]

---

# Professional interpretation

Lower EV/EBITDA can mean:

* Cheaper valuation
* Slower growth
* More cyclical business

Higher multiple can mean:

* Better business quality
* Higher growth
* Strong moat

---

# 5. Gross Margin

One of the MOST important quality metrics.

Formula:

[
Gross\ Margin = \frac{Revenue - COGS}{Revenue}
]

---

## Example comparisons

| Company                | Gross Margin   |
| ---------------------- | -------------- |
| Microsoft              | Very high      |
| NVIDIA                 | Extremely high |
| Hardware manufacturers | Lower          |

---

# Why professionals care

High margins often indicate:

* Pricing power
* Strong technology
* Strong moat
* Software-like economics

---

# 6. Free Cash Flow (FCF)

Professionals LOVE cash flow.

---

## Example

A company reports:

* Net income = $20B
* Free cash flow = $32B

That is VERY attractive.

Why?
Because cash is harder to fake than accounting earnings.

---

# FCF Yield

Formula:

[
FCF\ Yield = \frac{FCF}{MarketCap}
]

---

## Interpretation

| FCF Yield | Meaning               |
| --------- | --------------------- |
| Higher    | Potentially cheaper   |
| Lower     | Potentially expensive |

---

# 7. Return on Equity (ROE)

Formula:

[
ROE = \frac{NetIncome}{ShareholderEquity}
]

Measures:

> How efficiently management uses shareholder capital.

---

## Professional preference

Generally:

* > 15% = strong
* > 20% = excellent

But:

* Excessive debt can artificially inflate ROE

So professionals also check debt.

---

# 8. Revenue Growth

Critical for tech investing.

---

## Example

---

# Professional interpretation

Growth quality matters:

* Is it recurring?
* Sustainable?
* Profitable?

---

# 9. Operating Margin

Measures business efficiency.

Formula:

[
Operating\ Margin = \frac{OperatingIncome}{Revenue}
]

---

## Example

| Company Type   | Typical Margin |
| -------------- | -------------- |

| Retail         | Low            |

Professionals estimate:

Example:

* Long growth runway

# Key Beginner Mistake

Beginners often think:

> “Low PE = cheap”

Professionals know:

* Some stocks are cheap for good reasons
* Some expensive stocks are actually undervalued because growth is huge

That is why:

* PE alone is dangerous
* Context matters enormously
