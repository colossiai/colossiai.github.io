**Compare PE & PEG**

**PE (Price-to-Earnings Ratio)** and **PEG (Price/Earnings-to-Growth Ratio)** are both valuation metrics used for stocks, but they measure different things.

---

# PE Ratio

Formula:

[
PE = \frac{\text{Stock Price}}{\text{Earnings Per Share (EPS)}}
]

It tells you:

> “How much investors are paying for each $1 of company earnings.”

Example:

* Stock price = $100
* EPS = $5

[
PE = 100 / 5 = 20
]

Meaning:
Investors are willing to pay **20× annual earnings**.

---

## How to interpret PE

| PE      | Meaning                                       |
| ------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Low PE  | Possibly cheap / undervalued                  |
| High PE | Possibly expensive / high growth expectations |

But PE alone can be misleading.

Example:

* A fast-growing AI company may deserve PE 50.
* A slow/no-growth company with PE 50 may be overvalued.

---

# PEG Ratio

Formula:

[
PEG = \frac{PE}{\text{Earnings Growth Rate}}
]

It adjusts PE by growth.

Example:

* PE = 30
* Earnings growth = 15%

[
PEG = 30 / 15 = 2.0
]

---

## How to interpret PEG

| PEG | Common Interpretation   |
| --- | ----------------------- |
| < 1 | Potentially undervalued |
| ≈ 1 | Fairly valued           |
| > 1 | Possibly expensive      |

---

# Why PEG is useful

Two companies can have the same PE but very different growth.

| Company     | PE | Growth | PEG |
| ----------- | -- | ------ | --- |
| AI Startup  | 40 | 40%    | 1.0 |
| Old Telecom | 40 | 5%     | 8.0 |

Both have PE 40, but the AI company grows much faster.

PEG helps compare:

* growth stocks
* tech companies
* AI companies
* semiconductor companies

---

# Simple analogy

* **PE** = “price”
* **PEG** = “price adjusted for growth”

Think:

> PE asks “Is it expensive?”
>
> PEG asks “Is the growth worth the price?”

---

# Limitations

## PE problems

* Ignores growth
* Can be distorted by temporary earnings
* Useless if earnings are negative

## PEG problems

* Growth estimates may be wrong
* Different analysts predict different growth
* Less useful for cyclical industries (banks, commodities)

---

# Real-world usage

For mature companies:

* investors often focus on PE

For high-growth tech:

* PEG is usually more informative

Examples:

* NVIDIA
* Advanced Micro Devices
* Palantir Technologies

often trade at high PE because investors expect rapid future growth.

---

# Quick summary

| Metric | Measures                  | Best For         |
| ------ | ------------------------- | ---------------- |
| PE     | Price vs current earnings | Stable companies |
| PEG    | Price vs earnings growth  | Growth companies |

A stock can have:

* **high PE but good PEG** → expensive but justified
* **low PE but terrible growth** → cheap for a reason
